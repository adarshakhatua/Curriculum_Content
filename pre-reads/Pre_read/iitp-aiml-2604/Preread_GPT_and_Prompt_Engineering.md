You open a ticket with customer support about a billing error, and within seconds a chatbot responds — not with a generic apology, but with a clear, empathetic explanation that references your specific account details and offers a precise resolution. The response feels human, yet you know it was generated by an AI. You wonder: how did the model know exactly what to say, and why does it sometimes produce brilliant answers while other times it rambles, hallucinates facts, or refuses to follow simple formatting instructions?

The naive approach to using a large language model is to treat it like a search engine — type a question and hope for the best. But GPT does not retrieve answers; it generates them one token at a time, making probabilistic choices at every step. Without understanding how those choices are made and how to guide them, you are leaving the quality, consistency, and safety of your AI application to chance. The difference between a model that produces a perfectly formatted JSON response and one that fabricates plausible nonsense often comes down to a handful of carefully chosen parameters and a well-structured prompt.

That is where **GPT & Prompt Engineering** becomes essential.

What if you could design a prompt that reliably extracts structured data from 10,000 customer emails, or build an AI tutor that solves math problems step by step without making arithmetic errors, or deploy a code assistant that generates correct, idiomatic Python instead of plausible-looking junk? These capabilities are not about finding a "better model" — they are about understanding how the model thinks and speaking its language. The session ahead is the key that turns a generic LLM into a precision instrument under your control.

**GPT** (Generative Pre-trained Transformer) belongs to a family of models that generate text by predicting the next token, one step at a time. Unlike BERT, which uses an **encoder** architecture that processes the entire input bidirectionally, GPT uses a **decoder-only** architecture with **causal masking** — each token can only attend to the tokens before it, never the ones that follow. This seemingly simple constraint is what makes generation possible: the model produces token after token, each conditioned on everything it has written so far. But raw probability distributions are rarely useful on their own. The model assigns some probability to every word in its vocabulary, and your job is to decide how to sample from that distribution. That is where **temperature**, **top-k**, and **top-p** sampling come into play — parameters that control how greedy or creative the model should be. And once you understand the generation mechanism, the next layer is **prompt engineering**: the art of structuring inputs to produce reliable, useful outputs. From **zero-shot** prompts (just state your request) to **few-shot** prompts (provide examples) to **chain-of-thought** prompting (show your reasoning), the way you frame a request radically changes what the model produces. This session will also cover **structured output** — enforcing JSON mode, format constraints, and system prompts — so that you can integrate GPT into real applications, not just have a conversation.

In the **previous session**, you explored the **Transformer Architecture & BERT** — the encoder stack with self-attention, feed-forward layers, and layer normalisation that underpins bidirectional language understanding. The leap to GPT is smaller than it seems — GPT removes the encoder, keeps the decoder, and adds causal masking so that the model can only look backward during generation. Where BERT learns to understand language by seeing the full context, GPT learns to produce language by predicting what comes next. You are moving from comprehension to generation.

## Why GPT Generates One Token at a Time

The most important design decision in GPT is also the simplest to state: the model can only ever look at tokens to the left. This is called **causal masking**, and it is enforced by modifying the self-attention computation so that when the model computes the representation for token at position *i*, the attention scores for tokens at positions *j > i* are set to negative infinity before the softmax, effectively zeroing them out. This stands in direct contrast to BERT, whose bidirectional attention lets every token attend to every other token simultaneously. You might wonder: why cripple the model like that? The answer is that generation is inherently left-to-right. When GPT is producing a response, token *i* exists only after tokens 1 through *i−1* have already been produced. There is no future context to attend to because the future has not been written yet. Causal masking is not a limitation; it is a structural necessity for autoregressive generation.

The decoder-only architecture of GPT consists of stacked transformer decoder blocks, each containing a masked self-attention sublayer, a cross-attention sublayer (used when conditioning on a prompt), and a feed-forward network. At every step, the model takes the sequence of tokens produced so far, passes them through the decoder stack, and produces a probability distribution over the entire vocabulary for the next token. It then samples from that distribution — not always picking the most likely token, but using one of several **sampling strategies** — appends the chosen token to the sequence, and repeats. This is the **autoregressive** loop: the output at step *t* becomes part of the input at step *t+1*. Understanding this loop is essential because every prompt engineering technique you will learn — few-shot examples, chain-of-thought, system prompts — ultimately works by shaping the conditional probability landscape that the model navigates one token at a time.

## How Temperature, Top-k, and Top-p Shape Every Response

Imagine the model has just computed the probability distribution over the next token. In a vocabulary of 50,000 words, some tokens might have probabilities in the double digits ("the", "a", "is"), while thousands of others each carry a tiny fraction of a percent. How do you choose which token to emit? If you always pick the highest-probability token (greedy decoding), responses become repetitive, dull, and deterministic — the model plays it safe every time. If you sample blindly from the raw distribution, you get chaotic, nonsensical output. The art lies in between, and that is where the three sampling parameters come into play.

**Temperature** is a scaling factor applied to the logits (raw scores) before the softmax. A temperature of 1.0 leaves the distribution unchanged. A value below 1.0 (say 0.3) sharpens the distribution: high-probability tokens become even more likely, and low-probability tokens become vanishingly unlikely. A value above 1.0 (say 1.5) flattens the distribution, making unlikely tokens more competitive and injecting randomness. For tasks requiring factual precision — summarising a legal document, converting a date format — use low temperature. For creative writing, brainstorming, or generating diverse responses, use higher temperature. **Top-k** sampling goes a step further: it restricts the sampling pool to the *k* highest-probability tokens before applying temperature, forcing the model to ignore the long tail of improbable tokens. **Top-p** (nucleus) sampling is more adaptive: it selects the smallest set of tokens whose cumulative probability exceeds *p* (usually 0.9), then samples only from that set. This means that when the model is confident (one token dominates), it considers few alternatives; when it is uncertain (many tokens have similar probability), it considers more. In practice, top-p is often more robust than top-k because it adapts to the shape of the distribution rather than using a fixed cutoff. Mastering these three knobs is what separates a practitioner who gets consistent, useful output from one who blames the model for being "unreliable."

## Where Prompt Engineering Appears in Real Life

Prompt engineering is no longer a niche skill — it is a core competency for anyone building on top of large language models, and it manifests across nearly every industry. In **customer support automation**, companies use GPT with system prompts that specify brand voice, escalation rules, and response structure to power chatbots that handle thousands of tickets daily. A single well-crafted system prompt — "You are a helpful customer support agent for Acme Corp. Be concise, empathetic, and always suggest a next step. If you cannot resolve the issue, escalate to a human" — can reduce hallucination rates and improve customer satisfaction scores. In **software development**, developers use few-shot prompting with chain-of-thought reasoning to make GPT generate correct code for complex tasks. Instead of asking "Write a Python function to merge sorted lists," they provide an example with a step-by-step reasoning trace, and the model follows the pattern, producing code that compiles on the first try rather than code that looks plausible but fails. The same chain-of-thought technique is used in **healthcare** to generate clinical summaries from doctor notes — the model is prompted to first extract key entities (symptoms, medications, diagnoses), then organise them into a structured report with JSON output, ensuring the output is machine-parseable and can feed directly into electronic health record systems. In **legal technology**, firms use zero-shot and few-shot prompting with format constraints to generate contract clauses, summarise depositions, and identify potential conflicts across thousands of pages of documents — all by instructing the model to output in a specific schema. **Financial services** apply prompt engineering to automate report generation, where GPT must follow strict regulatory disclaimers, cite specific data sources, and output in a standardised XML format; this is achieved through a combination of system prompts (setting the regulatory context) and JSON mode (enforcing the output structure). Across every one of these domains, the underlying principles are identical: understand the model's autoregressive nature, control the randomness with sampling parameters, and design prompts that lead the model step by step toward the desired output.

## Interesting Questions for the Live Session

- If causal masking prevents a token from seeing future tokens during training, how does GPT handle bidirectional tasks like summarization, where understanding the full document is essential before generating a summary?

- Chain-of-thought prompting dramatically improves reasoning on math and logic problems — does the model actually "reason," or does the prompt structure simply prime it to produce tokens that look like a reasoning trace?
- Structured output (JSON mode) ensures machine-parseable responses, but what happens when the underlying model lacks the capability to follow the schema — is there a risk of silent failures where the model returns valid JSON with incorrect values?
